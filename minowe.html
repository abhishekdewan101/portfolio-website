Minowe A community connecting with American Indian culture through online language learning.

Project Description

Minowe is an online community dedicated to teaching and learning Ojibwe, an American Indian language. Its goal is to enable certain Peripheral American Indians (defined below) to better connect with their American Indian communities. Building on prior research in language education, the Minowe website and mobile app emphasize situation-based learning. Minowe introduces non-speakers to Ojibwe through the use of game-oriented lessons. It facilitates language learning by connecting fluent speakers and non-speakers via video chat. Minowe encourages collaboration by incorporating user-generated vocabulary into future lessons and activities.

Design Process

Initial Brainstorming

To help the team narrow down their choices in accordance to the theme of the CHI Student Design Competition of 2015, 20 minutes of the first team meeting were dedicated to performing the "100 ideas in 20 mins" brainstorm session. The purpose of this session was to allow the team members to feel comfortable presenting their ideas in a team setting and also to help the team narrow down choices for a project. In order to help people to speak about their ideas, it was decided before the start of the session that feedback would be saved till the end of the session.

User Interview and Analysis

After the inital brainstorming session, the team had decided to work with the Native american population and had conducted user interviews with the Sault St Marie Tribe that resides in the Upper Peninsula of Michigan. The user interviews were conducted in order to help the team to better understand the target population. The insights from the user interviews were then analyzed using an affinity wall.

Inital Design Solution

The affinity wall provided us with useful insight regarding cultural idenity and how peripheral native american people struggled to find their idenity. The design solution was aimed to solve this problem using language as the tool. Our research had indicated that speaking the native language was considered by many as a key indicator of cultural identity. To help the team gain some feedback on all the work that had been done, we took part in a peer feedback session, whereby people were allowed to look at our design solution and provide us with uninhibited feedback.

Wireframing

Based on the feedback, the inital design was overhauled and wireframes of the new design were created to perform the first round of usability testing. These wireframes were created using Axure RP and would go on to form the basis of the design mockups for the final design solution.

Final Design Mockups

The final process of this project was to visually overhaul the wireframes, by incoporating colors and patterns from Native American culture into the design. Careful consideration was taken not to overuse the colors and patterns, however inspiration from these colors and patterns were taken in order to appeal to our primary user group.
